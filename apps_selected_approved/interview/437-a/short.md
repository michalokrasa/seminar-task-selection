A child solves multiple-choice questions (A–D) using a fixed rule: a choice is "great" if its description length is at least twice shorter than every other choice's length, or at least twice longer than every other choice's length. If exactly one great choice exists, the child picks it; otherwise the child always answers C. Given the four answer descriptions, simulate and output the child's choice. The algorithmic approach is direct comparison across all four lengths; the main challenge is correctly implementing both halves of the "great" condition (len_i × 2 ≤ len_j for all j ≠ i, or len_j × 2 ≤ len_i for all j ≠ i) and ensuring exactly one such choice exists.
